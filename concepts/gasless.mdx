## The user experience

Your user wants to swap 100 USDC for ETH on Base. They have 100 USDC. They have **zero ETH**. They click "Swap", confirm with their passkey, and 30 seconds later they have ETH. No "first you need some ETH for gas" dance. No bridge from their bank.

That's the whole feature. The rest of this page is how it works under the hood — useful when you're debugging an integration, irrelevant for end users.

## Per-chain mechanism

| Chain family | Mechanism | Who pays gas |
|---|---|---|
| EVM (Ethereum, Polygon, Base, Optimism, Arbitrum, Avalanche) | [Biconomy MEE](https://biconomy.io) super-transactions + EIP-7702 user-level delegation | Fibo gas tank (per-chain ERC-20 funded sponsor account) |
| Solana | Backend fee-payer co-signature on V0 transactions | Fibo Solana relay account |
| Bitcoin | Standard input fee from the user's UTXO (no sponsorship) | The user (paid in BTC from the swap input) |

Bitcoin is the exception: there's no protocol-level analog to MEE on Bitcoin, and unspent-output gas dynamics make sponsorship economically irrational. For BTC outflows, the swap quote already includes the network fee; the user "pays gas" but it's invisible — it just looks like a slightly smaller output amount.

## How it nets out for Fibo's fees

Fibo's flat fee on every transaction (currently **0.5%**, deducted from the input amount) is sized to cover:

- The actual gas spent from the Fibo gas tank
- LiFi's integrator fee (paid back to Fibo as a rebate)
- A safety margin for gas-price volatility (Biconomy quote × 1.6)
- Fibo's margin

This is a transparent quote: `POST /v1/quote` returns the gross input, the net output the user receives, and an itemized `fees` breakdown. The user sees one number; you see all of it.

## What happens if the gas tank runs dry

Fibo monitors the gas tanks per chain and tops them up automatically. If a chain ever runs below the alerting threshold, two things happen:

1. **New quotes fail fast** on that chain with `503 gas_tank_low` — your SDK surfaces a clear error rather than letting the user sign a transaction that won't be relayed.
2. **In-flight transactions complete** — pending MEE super-transactions are not affected; they've already been quoted with sufficient gas.

Your webhook receives a `tenant.gas_tank_low` event so you can degrade gracefully (e.g., greying out the "Swap" button) in coordination with us. This is currently exception-rare; the gas tanks are monitored 24/7.

## Why not account abstraction (ERC-4337) directly?

A reasonable question if you've followed the AA space. The short answer: Biconomy's MEE adds two things over vanilla ERC-4337:

1. **EIP-7702 user-level delegation** — the user's EOA acts as a smart account *for one transaction* without permanently deploying a smart contract wallet at their address. After the transaction, the EOA is back to being a regular EOA. This sidesteps the "users now have a new address" problem of ERC-4337.
2. **Cross-chain super-transactions** — one MEE quote can include instructions across multiple chains (e.g., "swap USDC → ETH on Polygon, bridge ETH to Base"). The user signs once.

Biconomy's stack handles the bundler, paymaster, and entry-point complexity. Fibo's backend builds the MEE quote and submits it; you and your users see none of this.

## Solana fee-payer mechanism

Solana's V0 transaction format lets two parties sign the same transaction. Fibo's relay account is set as the `payerKey` server-side, the user signs the transaction as the operator, the backend co-signs as fee-payer, then submits. The user pays nothing in SOL.

This relies on the operator signature being the dominant one (which it is for any user-initiated swap), so the security model is identical to a normal Solana transaction: nothing happens without the user's signature. Fibo just absorbs the lamports.

## What you actually see in the API

You don't see any of this directly. `POST /v1/quote` returns a chain-agnostic quote; `POST /v1/submit` returns a `super_hash` (EVM) or a Solana `signature` or a Bitcoin `txid`. The webhook payload is uniform across all three. Gas figures appear in the `fees` breakdown for transparency; you can surface them or hide them as fits your product.
